Timbits

A cornerstone of Canadian popular culture, Timbits are the bite-sized doughnut holes sold at the iconic Tim Hortons coffee chain. They were first introduced in 1976 and quickly became a national favorite, symbolizing a quick and simple treat. They come in a wide variety of flavors, from classic glazed and sour cream to apple fritter and chocolate. Often sold by the box to be shared with friends, family, or colleagues, Timbits are a simple yet essential part of the Canadian experience.

All-Dressed Chips

All-Dressed chips are a uniquely Canadian sensation and a must-try for any snack connoisseur. The flavor is a wildly popular mix that combines several classic chip seasonings into one. You'll find notes of salty, savory, barbecue, sour cream, and even ketchup all in a single bite. This combination of sweet, salty, and tangy makes for an addictive and complex flavor profile that is a true reflection of Canadian snack culture.

Ketchup Chips

A snack food staple found almost exclusively in Canada, ketchup chips are a truly unique and tangy delight. These ridged potato chips are coated in a vibrant, savory seasoning that tastes uncannily like sweet and tangy ketchup. For many Canadians, it's a nostalgic flavor that brings back childhood memories and is a staple in pantries across the country. They are a must-try for any visitor looking to experience a truly Canadian snack classic.

Nanaimo Bars

Originating in the city of Nanaimo on British Columbia's Vancouver Island, the Nanaimo Bar is a classic no-bake dessert found in coffee shops and kitchens across Canada. It is composed of three distinct layers that create a perfect harmony of textures and flavors. The bottom layer is a rich, crumbly base of chocolate, coconut, and wafer crumbs. This is topped with a creamy, yellow custard-flavored buttercream. Finally, a thin layer of chocolate ganache is poured over the top, which hardens to provide a satisfying snap when you bite into it.

Smarties

In Canada, Smarties are not to be confused with the chalky, compressed sugar candies found in the United States or M&M's. Canadian Smarties are colorful, candy-coated chocolates that are a childhood favorite. Each piece has a distinctively crisp and firm shell that gives way to a smooth milk chocolate center. They are a beloved treat and a staple in Canadian convenience stores and movie theaters, easily recognizable by their vibrant, brightly colored shells.

Coffee Crisp

The Coffee Crisp is a chocolate bar that is a beloved Canadian icon. It features a unique combination of crispy vanilla wafer, foamed coffee-flavored candy, and a thin layer of rich milk chocolate. The bar’s light, airy texture and subtle coffee taste set it apart from other chocolate bars. It’s a classic treat that has been a favorite for generations, offering a simple yet satisfying blend of coffee and chocolate.

Mary Browns

Mary Brown's is a Canadian fast-food chain that has a dedicated following, especially in its home province of Newfoundland and Labrador where it was founded in 1969. Unlike many other fried chicken chains, Mary Brown's prides itself on using fresh, not frozen, chicken that is hand-breaded and cooked to order. The true star of the menu, however, is their signature "Taters." These are real, fresh-cut potato wedges that are fried to a golden-brown perfection, offering a crispy exterior and a fluffy interior. For many Canadians, a meal from Mary Brown's is a classic comfort food experience.

Maynards

A staple of Canadian candy aisles, Swedish Berries are a type of gummy candy made by the Maynards brand. While often confused with Swedish Fish, they are a uniquely Canadian treat with a different shape and a distinctly tangy, berry flavor. The gummies are shaped like raspberries and strawberries and have a soft, chewy texture that is instantly recognizable. You'll find these bright red candies in nearly every convenience store and movie theater across the country, making them a popular and nostalgic treat for many Canadians.

Nestlé

Nestlé's presence in Canada has led to a fascinating difference in chocolate compared to the Hershey-dominated American market. In the United States, Hershey holds the rights to produce many popular chocolate bars like Kit Kat, which are made with a different recipe. In Canada, Nestlé produces these same bars, resulting in a distinct taste. Canadian chocolate, including Nestlé's, is often described as having a richer cocoa flavor and being less sugary than its American counterpart. For many Canadians, the flavor profile is a point of national pride, as they believe their chocolate has a smoother, more balanced taste without the waxy mouthfeel some associate with American-made chocolates.

Canadian Cadbury

Like Nestlé, the Cadbury brand has a different recipe in Canada than it does in the United States, and in some ways, it is more similar to its British origins. In the U.S., the Hershey Company holds the license to manufacture many Cadbury products, and their recipe is known for being sweeter and having a distinct flavor. However, in Canada, Cadbury has its own production facilities and recipes. Canadian-made Cadbury chocolate is often praised for its creamier texture and a richer, more balanced cocoa taste, which many believe is a result of a higher milk and cocoa content. For many Canadians, this distinction makes a significant difference in taste, cementing the preference for their own domestically made bars like Dairy Milk and Caramilk.
